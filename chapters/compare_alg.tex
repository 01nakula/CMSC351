\section{Comparison of Sorting Algorithms}
\rule{\textwidth}{1pt}
\subsection{Temporal and Spatial Locality}
\subsubsection*{Temporal}
Temporal locality is dependent on how many variables you have and often
you have to replace them.
\begin{center}
	\begin{algorithmic}
		\State $sum \leftarrow 0$
		\State $j \leftarrow 1$
		\For{$i=1$ \textbf{to} $10$}
			\State $sum \leftarrow sum +j$
			\State $j \leftarrow j+j$
		\EndFor
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{center}
The above program has good temporal locality if there are three registers, 
but bad temporal locality if there are only two registers (there are three unique
variables in the program above so anything with less than three avaliable registers
will have bad temporal locality).

\subsubsection*{Spatial}
If a particular storage location is referenced at a particular time, then it is likely that
nearby memory locations will be referenced in the near future. In this case it is common 
to attempt to guess the size and shape of the area around the current reference for what it is
worthwhile to prepare faster access for subsequent reference.
